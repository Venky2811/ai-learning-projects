VENTURE CAPITAL (VC)

Venture Capital is a form of private equity financing provided to early stage startups 
and small businesses that show high growth potential. VC firms invest in companies that 
are too risky for traditional banks but have potential for massive returns.

VC firms raise money from limited partners such as pension funds, universities, and wealthy 
individuals. They then invest this money into startups in exchange for equity ownership. 
Typical VC investments range from $500,000 to $10 million in early stages.

The VC investment stages are Pre-seed, Seed, Series A, Series B, Series C and beyond. 
Pre-seed is the earliest stage where founders use personal savings or friends and family 
money. Seed stage is typically $500K to $2M for product development. Series A is $2M to 
$15M for scaling the business. Series B and beyond are for aggressive expansion.

Famous VC firms include Sequoia Capital, Andreessen Horowitz, Accel Partners and Tiger 
Global. Famous VC backed companies include Google, Facebook, Uber, Airbnb and Flipkart.

VC investors make money when portfolio companies get acquired or go public through an IPO. 
They typically expect 10x to 100x returns on successful investments because most startups 
fail. Out of 10 investments a VC might make, 7 fail, 2 do okay and 1 becomes a huge success.

HEDGE FUNDS

Hedge funds are investment funds that pool capital from accredited investors and use 
complex strategies to generate returns regardless of market conditions. Unlike mutual funds, 
hedge funds can use leverage, short selling, derivatives and other sophisticated instruments.

Hedge funds charge a 2 and 20 fee structure meaning 2 percent management fee on assets 
and 20 percent performance fee on profits. This makes hedge fund managers extremely wealthy 
if they perform well. Famous hedge funds include Bridgewater Associates, Renaissance 
Technologies and Citadel.

Hedge fund strategies include long short equity where they buy stocks they think will rise 
and short stocks they think will fall. Global macro strategy bets on macroeconomic trends 
like interest rates and currency movements. Quantitative strategies use algorithms and 
mathematical models to find trading opportunities.

Hedge funds are only open to accredited investors who have net worth over $1 million or 
annual income over $200,000. Minimum investments are typically $1 million or more. Hedge 
funds have less regulation than mutual funds but more than private equity.

PRIVATE EQUITY (PE)

Private Equity firms buy established companies, improve their operations and sell them 
for a profit. Unlike VC which invests in startups, PE firms typically buy mature companies 
that are underperforming or need restructuring.

PE firms use a strategy called Leveraged Buyout or LBO where they use a combination of 
their own capital and debt to buy companies. The acquired company's assets are used as 
collateral for the debt. This amplifies returns but also increases risk.

The PE investment process involves buying a company, improving operations over 3 to 7 years 
and then exiting through a sale or IPO. PE firms add value by cutting costs, improving 
management, expanding into new markets and making add-on acquisitions.

Famous PE firms include Blackstone, KKR, Carlyle Group and Apollo Global Management. 
PE firms typically target companies with stable cash flows, strong market position and 
potential for operational improvement.

PE professionals earn through management fees of 1.5 to 2 percent and carried interest 
of 20 percent of profits. Senior partners at top PE firms can earn tens of millions 
of dollars annually.

INVESTMENT BANKING (IB)

Investment Banking is a financial service that helps companies raise capital, execute 
mergers and acquisitions and provides financial advisory services. Unlike VC, PE and 
hedge funds, investment banks do not invest their own money in most cases.

Investment banks have multiple divisions. The mergers and acquisitions division advises 
companies on buying or selling other companies. The capital markets division helps 
companies raise money through stock offerings and bond issuances. The sales and trading 
division buys and sells securities on behalf of clients.

Famous investment banks include Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JP Morgan, Deutsche Bank 
and Barclays. These are called bulge bracket banks. Boutique investment banks like 
Lazard and Evercore focus specifically on advisory services.

Investment banking analysts work extremely long hours of 80 to 100 hours per week. 
The compensation starts at $100,000 to $150,000 for analysts fresh out of college. 
Senior bankers called Managing Directors can earn $1 million to $5 million annually.

Investment banking is often a stepping stone to PE and hedge funds. Many PE professionals 
start their careers as investment banking analysts to learn financial modelling and 
deal structuring skills.

KEY DIFFERENCES

The main difference between VC and PE is the stage of companies they invest in. VC invests 
in early stage startups while PE invests in mature established companies. VC takes minority 
stakes while PE often takes majority or full ownership.

The difference between hedge funds and PE is the investment horizon. Hedge funds trade 
frequently and can be in and out of positions within days or weeks. PE holds investments 
for 3 to 7 years before exiting.

Investment banking differs from all three because it is a service business not an 
investment business. IB earns fees for advising and facilitating transactions rather 
than investing capital and earning returns.

Risk levels differ significantly. VC is highest risk with potential for total loss but 
also highest potential returns of 100x. PE is medium risk targeting 2x to 5x returns. 
Hedge funds vary by strategy. IB has no investment risk but has reputational and 
regulatory risk.

Career paths often flow from IB to PE or hedge funds. Top MBA graduates join IB as 
associates. After 2 to 3 years they move to PE or hedge funds for better compensation 
and more interesting work. VC is often entered by former entrepreneurs or operators 
rather than bankers.
